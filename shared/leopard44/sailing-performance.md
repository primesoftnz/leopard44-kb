# Leopard 44 / Sunsail 444 — Sailing Performance

## Upwind Ability and Tacking

- **Close-hauled:** 6.5 knots in 13 knots of breeze, tacking through ~110 degrees
- **Apparent wind angle:** 35–50 degrees AWA maintaining 6–6.5 knots
- **Challenges:** VMG drops significantly when reefed or jib partially furled; uncomfortable to windward in heavy seas
- **Hull design:** "Tulip shape" — deep and narrow at waterline (minimizes wetted surface), flaring out above for volume. Double-stepped hulls maintain slimness at waterline.

## Speed in Various Conditions

| Condition | Speed |
|---|---|
| 10 kt true wind (120% genoa) | ~8 knots |
| 15 kt beam reach | 12+ knots |
| Top speed reported | 20 knots |
| Motoring at 2,800 rpm | ~8 knots |
| Motoring at 2,600 rpm | ~6 knots |

## Motion Comfort and Stability

- Super stable, non-heeling platform
- Motion in big seas somewhat "jerky" but very little roll vs monohulls
- Watertight bulkheads in bows and engine rooms for collision isolation
- Forward cockpit provides excellent visibility and airflow
- "Toe kicks" on exterior surfaces for tactile safety in rough conditions

## Polar Diagram Data

Raymarine has integrated Leopard-specific polar diagrams into Axiom navigation software. VPP targets based on boat weight, hull shape, and rig provide real-time comparison of actual vs projected speeds for sail trim optimization.

## Comparison to Similar Catamarans

- **Catana 42:** Faster, lighter (carbon/aramid), daggerboards for upwind; but exposed twin helms
- **Antares 44i:** Offshore-capable, protected helm; but galley-down layout
- **Leopard 46:** Similar seaway handling, more compact
- **Unique feature:** Forward cockpit with direct saloon access — set trend for modern cruising cats
